\section{Results}
This section reports the review results from the final set of included studies. The presentation begins with the corpus profile and then develops the findings according to the analytical directives used in extraction and synthesis.

\subsection{Corpus Profile and Scope of the Evidence}
The initial search retrieved 240 records: 105 from Scopus, 90 from IEEE Xplore, and 45 from Web of Science. Duplicate screening in Rayyan identified 119 repeated records. After duplicate resolution, 178 records remained for title and abstract screening, of which 129 were excluded and 49 moved to full-text retrieval. Full text was obtained for 26 records, whereas 23 could not be retrieved, including one retracted record. At the eligibility stage, 7 studies were excluded and 19 were retained for extraction and synthesis.

The flow of identification, screening, eligibility, and inclusion is summarized in the PRISMA diagram in Figure~\ref{fig:prisma_srl}.

The general characteristics of the publications included in the analysis are summarized in Figure~\ref{fig:sub_perfil_anual}. The annual distribution is concentrated between 2021 and 2026, with the highest density in 2022. Figure~\ref{fig:sub_perfil_tipo} shows a predominance of experimental and correlational/observational studies, whereas empirical quantitative studies without a specific subtype are less frequent. This configuration suggests a recent and empirically oriented corpus, suitable for the comparative characterization of implementation and evaluation practices within the period analyzed.


Figure~\ref{fig:sub_perfil_retrieval} shows that, among the 49 records sent to full-text retrieval, the largest group corresponds to obtained reports, followed by records in requested status; non-retrieved and retracted cases are a minority. This indicates sufficient documentary coverage to support the synthesis, with a fraction of records still dependent on retrieval. On the other hand, Figure~\ref{fig:sub_perfil_calidad} shows a balanced quality distribution between the high and medium bands, with very limited presence of low-band studies. This pattern suggests an intermediate-to-high confidence level in the set, while still requiring bias control because of design heterogeneity.

\subsection{Data and Variables for Air Quality Assessment (D1)}

This subsection reports the atmospheric pollutants and environmental variables used as inputs in the included studies, with attention to their frequency, typical combinations, and relation to the stated output objectives.

Table~\ref{tab:d1_resumen_frecuencias} shows that PM$_{2.5}$ and CO are the most frequently reported inputs in the corpus, each appearing in eight studies. PM$_{2.5}$ is used in studies centered on forecasting or concentration prediction \cite{A135,A157,A144} and also in calibration-oriented settings against reference stations \cite{A084}. CO appears more often in studies that classify AQI severity \cite{A009,A168} or combine indoor-air-quality and AQI categories in the same fuzzy scheme \cite{A167}. CO$_2$ and PM$_{10}$ form a second group of recurrent inputs.

Their use is tied to different input designs. CO$_2$ is usually part of multivariate configurations that combine gases and particulate matter to characterize indoor conditions \cite{A136} or to predict several pollutants jointly \cite{A135,A138}. PM$_{10}$ appears in forecasting settings \cite{A138} and in broader pollutant combinations used to estimate AQI or pollutant levels \cite{A028,A178}. TVOC and SO$_2$ also appear with visible frequency, while NO$_2$ is less recurrent. TVOC is used in studies focused on VOC prediction and operational decisions such as filter replacement \cite{A087}, and also in fuzzy indoor-air-quality indices \cite{A149}. SO$_2$ and NO$_2$ are more often embedded in multi-gas models for AQI reconstruction, AQI prediction, or joint pollutant-level assessment \cite{A001,A028}. Other inputs, including O$_3$, NOx, CH$_4$, NH$_3$, smoke, dust particles, and ultrafine particle counts, appear only in more specific settings, such as alarm-based environmental classification \cite{A114}, gas-alert systems \cite{A165}, or contamination-risk assessment in controlled environments \cite{A142}.

Table~\ref{tab:d1_resumen_frecuencias} summarizes this frequency pattern and shows that most studies rely on a relatively small group of recurrent inputs, particularly PM$_{2.5}$, CO, CO$_2$, and PM$_{10}$. Figure~\ref{fig:d1_frecuencia_y_cooc_entradas} adds the co-occurrence structure and shows that these variables are often used together in similar combinations. This can be seen in studies that work with pollutant sets such as PM$_{2.5}$/PM$_{10}$/CO/CO$_2$/NO$_2$/TVOC for forecasting or multi-output prediction \cite{A135,A138}, and in multi-gas schemes used for AQI estimation \cite{A028}.

In contrast, inputs such as NOx/NH$_3$/smoke appear in specific classification configurations under particular contexts \cite{A009}.

With respect to input dimensionality, Figure~\ref{fig:sub_num_contaminantes_por_estudio} shows the predominance of low- to medium-complexity configurations (one and two pollutants), together with a smaller subset of broad multivariate designs (four to six pollutants). Environmental variables appear mainly as contextual inputs that accompany pollutant measurements. Temperature and humidity are the most frequent, clearly above the other environmental descriptors. They are reported in multi-gas AQI reconstruction or prediction models \cite{A001,A028}, in particulate-matter forecasting studies \cite{A135,A138}, and in indoor-air-quality applications \cite{A136,A149}.

Other contextual variables are much less frequent. Atmospheric pressure, UV radiation, wind speed, and temporal attributes such as hour of day or day of week appear only in more specific input designs. Pressure and UV are reported in AQI prediction models with broader input sets \cite{A028} and in calibration-oriented settings \cite{A084}. Wind speed appears as an additional covariate in a limited number of cases \cite{A001}, and temporal variables are concentrated in PM$_{2.5}$ prediction models where hourly and weekly patterns are part of the feature design \cite{A157}. The co-occurrence results show that PM$_{2.5}$, CO, and CO$_2$ are the pollutants most often combined with temperature and humidity.

Figure~\ref{fig:variables_ambientales_salida} relates these environmental inputs to the output classes reported by the studies. Temperature appears across the main output groups, especially AQI/IAQ estimation, pollutant prediction, and classification. Humidity follows a similar pattern, with recurrent use in estimation and prediction and lower presence in classification. This distribution is compatible with the dominant outputs in the corpus, which include AQI prediction \cite{A009,A028}, index reconstruction or estimation \cite{A001,A167}, PM forecasting \cite{A135,A138,A157}, and alert-enabled classification \cite{A114,A165}.

In the prediction output class, Figure~\ref{fig:contaminantes_por_salida} shows a clear concentration around PM$_{2.5}$, followed by TVOC, CO$_2$, PM$_{10}$, and NO$_2$. This distribution matches the input structures reported in studies such as \cite{A135}, where PM$_{2.5}$ forecasting is carried out together with gases and VOC-related variables, and \cite{A138}, where PM$_{10}$ forecasting uses combinations of particulate matter, CO$_2$, and TVOC. For AQI/IAQ estimation, a more balanced distribution is observed. CO, CO$_2$, TVOC, SO$_2$, and NO$_2$ present similar frequencies, which is compatible with approaches in which the objective is to reconstruct/estimate a composite index from multiple pollutants \cite{A001}, \cite{A028}, \cite{A149}, \cite{A178}. By contrast, calibration tasks show an exclusive focus on PM$_{2.5}$, coherent with studies whose explicit objective is concentration adjustment and calibration against a reference \cite{A084}. Contamination-risk evaluation appears punctually associated with specific variables such as ultrafine particle counts, which define risk groups \cite{A142}.


The heatmap in Figure~\ref{fig:relacion_salida_contaminantes} confirms these patterns by showing higher intensities for the CO--classification and PM$_{2.5}$--prediction combinations. It also shows that index estimation presents moderate co-occurrences distributed among several pollutants instead of depending on only one. Less frequent output classes show dispersed and low values, which matches more specialized objectives, such as the hybrid LoP/m-AQI index based on specific pollutants (e.g., benzene) \cite{A178} or operational decision support (actuators) in environmental classification scenarios \cite{A114}, \cite{A123}, \cite{A132}.

Figure~\ref{fig:contaminantes_vs_salida} integrates this reading by combining frequency and co-occurrence of pollutants according to output type.

Pollutant selection is not homogeneous across output classes. Classification relies more frequently on CO, prediction prioritizes PM$_{2.5}$ and multivariate combinations, whereas index estimation integrates several pollutants with similar intensities. The corpus also confirms that this selection is accompanied by preprocessing practices coherent with data type and objective: calibration and normalization in reconstruction/estimation schemes \cite{A001}, \cite{A167}; cleaning, imputation, and \emph{feature engineering} in PM forecasting \cite{A135}, \cite{A138}; synchronization and imputation in calibration against reference stations \cite{A084}; and specific strategies such as weekly aggregation \cite{A009} or sliding windows \cite{A157} when the approach is temporal. This behavior suggests that the functional objective of the system conditions both the configuration of input variables and the data treatment, reinforcing the methodological consistency of the reviewed real-time monitoring platforms.

\subsection{Fuzzy-Logic Approach and Design Decisions (D2)}
This section examines how the fuzzy component is implemented within the analyzed systems, organizing the synthesis into four layers: configuration distributions, co-occurrence relationships among design decisions, compact profiles by FIS family, and minimum traceability by study.

In the first layer, Table~\ref{tab:d2_tabla_principal} consolidates the quantitative evidence (k/n/\%) for the eight dimensions of D2. The corpus shows a shared predominance of \texttt{ANFIS} and \texttt{Mamdani} (\texttt{7/19}; \texttt{36.8\%} each), with an almost total central fuzzy role (\texttt{18/19}; \texttt{94.7\%}). In inference purpose, AQI classification (\texttt{12/19}; \texttt{63.2\%}), risk alerting (\texttt{8/19}; \texttt{42.1\%}), and pollutant prediction (\texttt{7/19}; \texttt{36.8\%}) prevail \cite{A084, A132, A149, A168}. In reporting completeness, the main gap remains defuzzification (\texttt{NR=8/19}; \texttt{42.1\%}), whereas coverage is higher in the other dimensions.

\input{assets/tablas/results/d2/d2_tabla_principal_es.tex}

Table~\ref{tab:d2_tabla_principal} fixes the marginal counts by dimension (k/n/\%), whereas Figure~\ref{fig:d2_tipos_y_proposito} is used to visualize the cross-relationship between FIS family and inference purpose. The dominant pattern places \texttt{Mamdani} in classification/alert scenarios and \texttt{ANFIS} in configurations oriented toward prediction and operational optimization \cite{A114, A123, A149, A084, A087, A168}. This difference reflects two design styles, one centered on explicit linguistic rules and the other more oriented toward parametric adjustment.

As the closing of the first descriptive layer, Figure~\ref{fig:d2_aqi_resumen} summarizes, based on AQI class count and label fields, the AQI class configurations reported: number of classes per study and most frequent labels. In the corpus (\texttt{n=15} with explicit labeling), a nominal convergence is observed in low-exposure base labels (\texttt{Good}, \texttt{Moderate}) and a progressive divergence in higher-severity labels. In frequency, \texttt{Good} (\texttt{12/15}) and \texttt{Moderate} (\texttt{11/15}) predominate, whereas \texttt{Unhealthy} and \texttt{Hazardous} appear in \texttt{8/15} studies; this indicates that cross-study agreement is greater at low/mid levels than in the high-risk zone.

This heterogeneity is better understood by comparing labeling schemes. A first group adopts taxonomies close to the classic AQI with 5--6 classes (\texttt{Good/Moderate/.../Hazardous}), as in \cite{A001, A009, A135, A167, A168}. A second group uses semantic variants to refine local risk interpretation, such as \texttt{Sensitive/Harmful} \cite{A149}, \texttt{Very Poor} \cite{A136}, or \texttt{Good/Medium/Bad/Very Bad/Dangerous} \cite{A123}. A third group encodes risk in ordinal or proprietary scales, such as \texttt{Low/Medium/High} \cite{A178}, \texttt{NAP/LAP/HAP} levels \cite{A132}, or \texttt{RG0--RG13} gradients \cite{A142}. In design terms, this mix of labels describes the same functional idea (risk stratification for decision and alerting), but with different granularity and different operational semantics; therefore, comparison across studies requires harmonizing the meaning of the classes before harmonizing only their number.

In the second layer, Figures~\ref{fig:d2_fis_defuzz} and \ref{fig:d2_fis_riesgo} reveal coupling patterns among FIS family, defuzzification method, and risk-modeling strategy \cite{A001, A009, A123, A149, A114, A142, A178}. These figures indicate that the choice of FIS family conditions model output and the way results are explained and audited.

The cross-reading of these co-occurrences shows a recurrent pattern: \texttt{Mamdani} designs are more often linked to AQI-threshold-based classification and centroid defuzzification, whereas \texttt{ANFIS} appears more often in studies oriented toward prediction, optimization, and adaptive adjustment \cite{A001, A009, A084, A138, A149, A168}. This distinction is visible in the way the reviewed studies distribute fuzzy families across different output objectives.

System size also varies considerably across studies. Some proposals report large rule bases, such as 138 rules in \cite{A135} and 576 rules in \cite{A142}, whereas others use more compact structures, such as 24 rules in \cite{A149} or 36 rules in \cite{A167}. These differences show that studies with related purposes may still adopt very different levels of rule-base complexity.

Figure~\ref{fig:d2_proposito_interpretabilidad} shows that AQI classification and risk-alerting studies more often report explicit interpretability elements such as rule transparency or AQI mapping, whereas prediction-oriented studies more often report membership-function settings and adjustment procedures \cite{A149, A165, A167, A084, A135}. The figure therefore separates two common reporting patterns: one centered on explanation of rules and categories, and another centered on model configuration and adjustment.

Optimization and adjustment are also reported through different procedures: PSO/GA combinations for performance calibration \cite{A138}, evolutionary adjustment through DE \cite{A178}, and rule reduction or adjustment schemes in ANFIS settings \cite{A084}. Taken together, the D2 evidence places fuzzy inference in a central role within the reviewed systems, mainly in classification, alerting, and prediction tasks, while also showing differences among FIS families in rule bases, membership functions, defuzzification, and interpretability elements.

The comparative reading of these profiles shows two clearly differentiated design patterns. On the one hand, \texttt{Mamdani} and \texttt{Sugeno/TSK} concentrate more stable and traceable configurations, with central role, predominance of AQI classification, and explicit rule bases; on the other hand, \texttt{ANFIS} shows greater orientation toward optimization and adaptive adjustment, with greater variability in membership functions and output mechanisms \cite{A009, A123, A132, A149, A084, A138}.

The overall exploration of D2 shows a predominance of architectures with a central fuzzy core and a balanced presence of \texttt{Mamdani} and \texttt{ANFIS}, confirming that fuzzy logic acts as the main component in AQI classification, alerting, and, to a lesser extent, prediction \cite{A009, A132, A149, A168}. At the same time, partial convergence is observed in explicit rule bases and interpretability, with relevant variation in membership functions, defuzzification, and risk modeling \cite{A001, A123, A144, A149, A178}; therefore, comparison across studies requires clearly formulating common factors and family-specific decisions in order to maintain traceability and comparability.

\subsection{Platform and Real-Time Operation (D3)}
This section analyzes how real-time air-quality monitoring platforms are implemented, with emphasis on their architecture, processing mode, and operation. The purpose is to identify recurrent technical configurations and evaluate their coherence across technological stack, deployment context, instrumentation, and visualization/alerting layer, maintaining explicit traceability between article-level evidence and synthesized statements.

\paragraph{Architecture and Technologies Used}
The distribution of architecture and processing mode in this subsection is grounded in Figures~\ref{fig:d3_sub_distribucion_arquitectura}, \ref{fig:d3_sub_distribucion_procesamiento}, and \ref{fig:d3_cooc_arquitectura_procesamiento}. Based on this evidence, an IoT implementation core is observed, with predominance of \texttt{IoT edge-cloud} (\texttt{6/19}; \texttt{31.6\%}) and \texttt{Two-layer IoT} (\texttt{5/19}; \texttt{26.3\%}), together with a smaller band of alternative architectures (\texttt{WSN channel}, \texttt{Sensor node with external analytics}, and \texttt{Independent node with PC}). In the temporal dimension, the most frequent mode is \texttt{Near-real-time} (\texttt{7/19}; \texttt{36.8\%}), followed by strict real-time operation when considering \texttt{Real time at the edge} and \texttt{Distributed real time} together (\texttt{6/19}; \texttt{31.6\%}), with additional presence of hybrid configurations (\texttt{4/19}; \texttt{21.1\%}).

Reporting completeness in D3 remains generally high. The most visible gaps appear in visualization and alerting, and to a lesser extent in platform architecture and communication/technology, whereas deployment context is fully reported in the corpus. Rather than an independent result, this observation supports the interpretation of how far the technical configurations described can be followed with sufficient traceability.

\paragraph{Real-Time Operation and Deployment}
This part analyzes how the technology used, the deployment setting, and the operational mode are combined in order to identify recurrent patterns across studies.

Figure~\ref{fig:d3_cooc_arquitectura_stack} shows a concentration of co-occurrences between \texttt{IoT edge-cloud} and \texttt{Cloud server}, \texttt{WiFi}, and \texttt{MATLAB} (\texttt{k=4}). In the corpus, this result corresponds to a recurrent technical sequence: local node acquisition, transmission toward cloud infrastructure, and analytical processing in software. Article-level evidence maintains this structure with implementation variations: in \cite{A114}, the ESP32 transmits via WiFi to Firebase for online monitoring and FIS+DRL decision support; in \cite{A135}, Arduino+ESP8266 sends periodic data to ThingSpeak and analysis is integrated in MATLAB; in \cite{A009}, telemetry recorded in ThingSpeak is used in MATLAB for fuzzy inference; and in \cite{A138}, the IoT-to-ThingSpeak flow is complemented with preprocessing in MATLAB through imputation and normalization prior to forecasting.

Beyond the dominant block, the tail of unique occurrences contributes technical signals worthy of analysis. In \cite{A142}, the combination \texttt{Python}+\texttt{Google Colab}+\texttt{Bluetooth}/\texttt{LAN Gateway} appears in a clinical IAQ monitoring scenario for operating rooms with fuzzy DSS; in \cite{A167}, \texttt{WiFi}+\texttt{I2C}+\texttt{Google Sheets} is used for real-time collection and web visualization with alerting; in \cite{A168}, \texttt{Zigbee}+\texttt{HDFS MapReduce}+\texttt{Google Map} supports distributed monitoring and routing services; and in \cite{A178}, the integration of \texttt{Bluetooth}/\texttt{Zigbee}/\texttt{WiFi} with \texttt{AWS EC2} is framed within an IoT-Fog architecture for distributed operation. These configurations do not change the main pattern of Figure~\ref{fig:d3_cooc_arquitectura_stack}, but they do show that the choice of technological stack responds to domain and deployment requirements. This relationship is examined directly in Figure~\ref{fig:d3_cooc_contexto_instrumentacion}.

Figure~\ref{fig:d3_cooc_contexto_instrumentacion} shows two differentiated instrumentation profiles according to deployment setting. In \texttt{Indoor} settings, the highest density is concentrated in \texttt{PM sensor} \texttt{(k=9)}, \texttt{temperature-humidity sensor} \texttt{(k=8)}, and \texttt{CO sensor} \texttt{(k=7)}. These combinations appear in studies with multivariable indoor monitoring \cite{A135}, cloud-connected indoor-state prediction \cite{A136}, and clinical monitoring with particle counts and environmental variables \cite{A142}. In \texttt{Urban}/\texttt{Outdoor} settings, combinations involving \texttt{CO sensor} and \texttt{Arduino MCU} appear more often (\texttt{k=6} in urban settings for both). These cases include WSN-based distributed sensing with route-support services \cite{A168}, LoRa nodes with edge classification and field alert transmission \cite{A165}, and deployments evaluated under urban and desert conditions \cite{A001}.

Less frequent configurations also help characterize the range of implementation settings. In clinical environments, \cite{A142} combines \texttt{BLE infrastructure}, a \texttt{particle counter}, and microclimatic variables to monitor operating rooms and estimate risk groups. In mixed indoor/outdoor deployments, \cite{A165} integrates a \texttt{LoRa transceiver} and \texttt{Raspberry Pi SBC} with CO/CO$_2$/CH$_4$ sensors and tests the system in laboratory and public-road conditions. In another case, \cite{A001} reports validation under both urban and desert conditions with NO$_2$/SO$_2$/CO$_2$ sensing, whereas \cite{A136} extends indoor-air-quality monitoring to educational settings with PM/CO/CO$_2$ inputs and thermal variables.

This reading delimits the physical implementation layer: where the system is deployed and with which sensors it operates. The next step is to review how this data flow is translated into continuous operation for the end user, especially in terms of visualization and alert response. Figure~\ref{fig:d3_cooc_tiempo_real_visualizacion} indicates which outputs are more frequent and also makes it possible to distinguish two operational behaviors in the studies: continuous monitoring with visualization (\texttt{Continuous loop}+\texttt{Dashboard}) and monitoring with threshold-triggered activation (\texttt{Threshold alert}+\texttt{Alarm module}).

In the \texttt{Continuous loop}+\texttt{Dashboard} block \texttt{(k=7)}, studies describe continuous monitoring with visualization for operational follow-up: in \cite{A114}, the system transmits real-time data to Firebase and presents them through app/web interfaces while the FIS+DRL module updates control decisions; in \cite{A136}, the context-aware architecture uses IoT cloud services for analysis/prediction and delivers information together with alerts and recommendations to the user; and in \cite{A167}, the platform records CO, temperature, humidity, and PM in real time in a web application based on Google Sheets. In the \texttt{Threshold alert}+\texttt{Alarm module} block \texttt{(k=5)}, the activation logic is explicit: \cite{A167} defines the rule “if CO AQI exceeds dangerous level, activate alarm”, \cite{A165} reports early alerts with periodic frames of ID/time/sensors every 30 seconds, \cite{A142} issues warning messages according to risk groups in operating rooms, and \cite{A114} integrates buzzer and ventilation/AC actuation under environmental deterioration. The observed co-occurrence represents a transition from descriptive monitoring to operation with automatic condition-based response.

Less frequent cases preserve the same functional logic, but with more specialized output configurations in clinical settings, field alerting, and context-specific actuation.

The evidence shows that the output layer serves two complementary functions: continuous observability of the environment and operational response under critical conditions. This distinction helps interpret the corpus results, because studies with passive visualization and studies with alerting/actuation do not represent the same functional level of implementation.

\subsection{Performance Results (Cross-Synthesis of D1--D3)}
To support valid comparisons, this performance synthesis is structured into three conceptual planes: the type of result evaluated by each study (comparable unit), the nature of the reported metric (performance, error, or operational behavior), and the validation scheme used. This framing avoids direct contrasts between works that pursue different objectives or quantify performance with non-equivalent measures.

Based on this distinction, the analysis first identifies which groups in the corpus can be compared and how frequent they are. It then examines which metrics are reported in those groups and how their values are distributed.

Table~\ref{tab:eval_unidades_comparables} shows a core of four comparable units with equal weight (\texttt{3/19} each; \texttt{12/19} in total, \texttt{63.2\%}) and a tail of low-frequency units (\texttt{7/19}; \texttt{36.8\%}). The difference among these four units is operational: in \textit{AQI classification}, the output is a discrete class of air status; in \textit{AQI/IAQ estimation}, the output is a continuous index value; in \textit{IAQ classification+control}, classification is combined with actuator response or control action; and in \textit{PM2.5 prediction}, the output is a future particulate matter concentration.

These differences are reflected in metric selection and in the way results are interpreted by comparable unit.
